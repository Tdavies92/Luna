MorningSet the Stage
  • Wake baby no later than 7:30am (even if you have to rouse her) Foundation
  • Get at least 30 min outdoor time in the morning — natural light improves sleep patterns
  • Spend non-sleep time in baby's cot room — room acclimatisation, at least 30 min of play/activity
  • Bring baby into cot in mid-air, lower slowly — 'bop' sound, peek-a-boo if she's open to it
DaytimeNaps & Environment
  • Follow age-appropriate feeding/sleeping timetable — achieve naps as you normally would today (feed/rock/buggy fine) Day 1–2 only
  • Place security item (muslin/breathable blanket) in cot with baby each sleep
  • Room: blackout blinds closed, quiet (white noise if using), remove distractions from cot view
  • Afternoon outdoor activity — 30 min fresh air recommended
  • 20 min connected, undivided one-to-one play time (face-to-face, on the floor, let baby lead)
EveningBedtime Routine
  • Turn off TV/screens 1 hour before bedtime Every night
  • Quiet the house — move to living room/kitchen, one-to-one time
  • Finish any feeds (aim 45 min before sleep start)
  • Quick face wash & teeth brush (even if no teeth yet), go to baby's bedroom
  • Dim lights, pull curtains — room dark & quiet or white noise on
  • Dress baby in sleeping bag + appropriate layers for 16–20°C
  • Read/sing quietly — same words each time (e.g. "Sleep time now, [name]…")
  • 20–30 min wind-down, then place baby in cot awake & relaxed
  • Say key phrase ("I love you, it is time for sleep") — lights off, begin stay-and-support
NightStay-and-Support in Action
  • Position yourself beside cot/bed on floor (or low chair) — make yourself comfortable Every waking
  • Physically soothe: stroke, rub, pat, swirl hands over body — intermittently, not constantly
  • Verbally reassure: shush, hum, "broken record" phrase — maintain eye contact, stay calm
  • If standing: teach lie-down with key words + gentle pressure on mattress, encourage not demand
  • Stay until baby is fast asleep before leaving room
  • At any overnight waking: return to cot-side and repeat the same approach
Lucy Says

On day one, operate your usual timetable and achieve naps as you normally would. Make changes from dinnertime onwards — start bedtime with the new approach and operate it overnight. From day two onwards, begin naps in the cot with the new approach.

Based on The Baby Sleep Solution by Lucy Wolfe. For a breastfed baby approaching 6 months. Not medical advice — always follow your GP's guidance.

🛏️
For your situation

At 6 months, the ideal is baby's own cot in their own room. Studies show children sleep better in their own space once age-appropriate. The best time to move is between 6–8.5 months — limited object permanence means the transition is smoother now than it will be later.

Lucy Says

If you're moving baby to their own room, consider having one parent room-share with baby for the first 3 nights of the process to bridge the gap — especially useful if you've been bed-sharing.

The Stay-and-Support Approach

You remain present beside the cot, physically and verbally comforting your baby as she learns to settle independently. No leaving her alone to cry. No picking up unnecessarily. This is a gradual, attended sleep learning process.

Step by stepAt Bedtime & Overnight
🌙

Position yourself

▼

Get down to your child's level — on the floor beside the cot, or a chair if her cot is still at the high level. Make yourself comfortable. You may be there a while! Stay low and calm.

🤲

Physically respond

▼

Touch baby intermittently, not constantly (to avoid creating a new hand-holding dependency). Techniques to try:

  • Stroke the bridge of the nose
  • Rub the back of the neck
  • Pat/shush on their side or torso
  • Walk fingers up their body
  • Swirl hands over their tummy
  • Wiggle their torso / roll them
  • Do a tom-tom rhythm on their side
Lucy Says

If baby grabs your hand, pat or rub a different part of their body. Avoid letting them fall asleep holding your hand or finger — put it in their security item instead.

💬

Verbally respond

▼

Shush, hum, sing softly. Use the "broken record" technique — repeat the same soothing phrase over and over: "It's sleep time, Harry, night night…" or sing a favourite song. Maintain eye contact. Stay in the room, dark as outlined — your child can see you.

🔀

Use distraction

▼

If physical touch and words aren't calming, try distraction — a different song, a funny noise, drawing baby's attention to something else. Crying and sleep are not related. Crying is typically a protest; your job is to bring the crying wave down so baby can settle.

🧍

If baby stands up

▼

Very few children under 9 months will stand. If she does:

  • During the day practise going from standing to sitting/lying
  • Ring-a-Rosy and "all fall down" games help
  • Use key words: "lie down", "all fall down"
  • First allow her to get out of system, do a lap of honour of the cot
  • After, if still standing: come low, encourage to come down on their own
  • Pat the mattress and stay down yourself
  • If truly stuck: put them down once, return to low position
🤗

To pick up or not?

▼

The answer is almost always no — but: if baby becomes hysterical, pick up, hold only until calm (about 20–30 min), then place back down. Never allow them to fall asleep in your arms as this creates a new dependency. Stay and return to cot-side techniques.

🚪

Don't leave

▼

Stay with your child until they are fast asleep. Don't leave the cot/bedroom until breathing is regular and they are deeply asleep. After ten minutes is normally safe. Do this at bedtime and during all night wakings — leave too soon and they will hyper-vigilant, waking constantly.

RememberKey Principles
⏱️ The first 7–10 days are the most challenging. Most parents report improvements in mood, behaviour and appetite — and feel more confident and capable — once their child develops this new skill set. It does get easier.
✅ Be consistent. Every single sleep — nap and night — same approach, same response. Intermittent reinforcement makes the process longer.
💕 Both parents interchangeable is ideal. Alternate nights one, two & then the other. If just one parent — that's fine too. You are fully capable of this.
Quick referenceBefore You Start
📋

Pre-start checklist

▼
  • ✅ Get green light from GP — baby is 100% well
  • ✅ Not during/just after vaccinations
  • ✅ Set aside 3–4 weeks — no big changes, sleepovers, or holidays
  • ✅ Start on a Friday night if possible (you 'own' Sat/Sun)
  • ✅ Commit: don't start, stop, then restart
  • ✅ Baby is not chronically teething or running a temperature
🏠

Sleep environment setup

▼
  • Room adequately dark — blackout blinds essential, especially summer
  • Temperature: 16–20°C — check and adjust clothing accordingly
  • Use a night light (4W max) only — no hall lights, bathroom lights, lamps on dimmer switch
  • Remove distractions from cot view: mobiles, mirrors, wardrobe doors open, clothes on floor
  • Baby monitor positioned from across the room, out of reach
  • Use sleeping bag — prevents blanket kicking and supports cot safety
🧸

The security item

▼

Introduce a safe, breathable security item — a piece of muslin or small breathable blanket. Wear it against your skin for a night or two, then place in cot. Baby will associate your scent with the sleeping environment. Choose something: safe (100% breathable), washable, and have multiples.

Lucy Says

A handkerchief-sized piece of muslin is ideal — safe and the right size. Have several so you always have a fresh one available.

🎵

White noise / music

▼

White noise can be very effective at 6 months — acts as a sound barrier and distraction. Use it during the bedtime routine and throughout the sleep period. Turn it off after 15–20 min once asleep, unless you plan to use it all night. Avoid making it a sleep association you must maintain forever — wean off when sleep is established.

🍼

Breastfeeding & this process

▼

You do not need to stop breastfeeding. The goal is to separate the feed-to-sleep association, not remove feeds. Aim to finish feeds at least 45 minutes before placing baby in cot so she is awake (not drowsy) when she goes down. Night feeds can continue as needed — simply follow the same stay-and-support approach after feeding, placing her back in the cot awake.

Lucy Says

If baby is breastfed, it may be best for dad to do the room-sharing during the transition nights, with mum available for feeds as required. This prevents baby smelling the breast and expecting to feed to sleep.

😴

Dummy guidance

▼

A dummy is a sleep enabler at this age, not a dependency you need to remove yet. At around 8 months most babies can learn to use it independently. For now: offer it, don't actively take it away. Reduce re-plugs over time. After 8 months, teach baby to find and replace it themselves. Around 9 months a great night's sleep would be one to three re-plugs.

📖

Keeping a sleep log

▼

Record daily: what time baby woke, nap times and lengths, bedtime, night wakings and duration, your approach, and overall quality. This helps you objectively track progress week by week. Use the Sleep Log tab in this app to record entries.
